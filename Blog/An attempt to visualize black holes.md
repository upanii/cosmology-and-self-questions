## Attempts to Visualize a Black Hole

Attempts to capture a black hole lead to quite serious problems. Without additional study, when viewing images from professional stations that study various cosmic bodies, it might seem that everything shining in the sky are separate star systems with their own suns, some closer and brighter, some dimmer.

![Example](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/DESI_Legacy_Surveys_image_of_III_Zw_2.jpg)

- Imagine a light bulb with a black circle that will be visible to an observer at close range. Moving the bulb away, we see less and less of the black circle, and the bulb itself shines as if nothing is blocking it.

Using the light bulb example, mentally take an actual black hole. Moving it millions of light years away, it becomes similar to a sun, while changing the trajectory of light through 'gravitational lensing' by both the black hole itself and as the light reaches the observer, which is described in GR ('general relativity'). Thus, it's difficult to calculate the object without further study.

Let's return to the attempt at visualization. Visualizing a supermassive object that "absorbs matter into itself" is quite difficult. It should look justified from both a scientific and mathematical point of view. Let's start with a black hole and 'gravitational lensing'. It would look something like this:

![Example simple](https://naked-science.ru/wp-content/uploads/2021/10/original_df3fdef6f7163d7532eff34b9de7b55d.png)

A black hole without an 'accretion disk'. If we add an 'accretion disk' of gas and dust to this black hole, we get something like this:

![Example close to reality](https://naked-science.ru/wp-content/uploads/2019/10/chernaya-dyra-549977-G9Ga7puJ.jpg)

It looks beautiful, but it seems to me (logically) that it looks unnatural, or rather, the upper and lower reflection of the 'photon rings' should look thinner. But these are rather my reflections and an attempt to mentally imagine a black hole.

Next is a photo of the actual black hole 'Messier 87', taken in 2019, where the 'event horizon' and 'accretion disk' of the black hole are clearly visible.

![2019 photo](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Black_hole_-_Messier_87.jpg/1280px-Black_hole_-_Messier_87.jpg)

The main factor that needs to be taken into account is the angle of the observed object. If we look directly at the object, we get that same black hole, with a central 'accretion disk' and its reflection above and below. If we look at a black hole that is turned with one of its poles toward us, then visually it will be similar to our example with a light bulb, where there is light that is blocked in the middle by a black circle.
